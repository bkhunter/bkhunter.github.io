Sensory Underload

January - April 2016

An assignment in a Computers and Games course, Sensory underload is a game made with the restriction of no visual elements. Although a small project, it was a very interesting challenge to develop a sound-only mechanic in a small amount of time.

Overview

The same course that produced keith to the past also included some small game development assignments for teams to complete. These assignments were neat, but with a much larger project looming only limited time could be allocated to them. For this reason, Sensory Underload was completed over a weekend by another team member and me. Since we were both familiar with it, we used Python-Pygame to develop the game, which was a great time-saver but limited us to keyboard input. The game is similar to Temple Run, you try to stay alive as long as you can while running in a jungle avoiding obstacles. Except in this case, the obstacles were auditory instead of visual: a panther howl to dodge left or right, crumbling floor to jump, a whoosh for a quick duck, and a bird flock for a long slide. If you successfully dodge you hear a light chime, while failure sounds a harsher bloop. The type of obstacle is random throughout the game, but as you run the frequency increases, making the game more difficult. Fail three times and the game is over.

Our initial idea was to simulate the experience of someone who fell into a pitch black cave and must now run from what sounds like a monster as they try to escape. However, soon after implementing some base functionality we realized that a game like this would require much more developed mechanics not to mention very hard to acquire sounds/music. So we shifted towards more of a reactionary style, as described previously. One novel thing implemented in this project was audio panning, how sound can be distributed between the left and right audio channels. In summary, we made a basic game that is fun to play and was interesting to make.
